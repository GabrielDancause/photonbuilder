Why Component Allocation Matters

Building a gaming PC isn't just about throwing money at the most expensive parts; it's about eliminating bottlenecks. If you pair an extremely high-end processor with a low-tier graphics card for a gaming build, you'll leave a significant amount of performance on the table. Proper budget allocation ensures that every dollar spent contributes directly to a smoother, higher-framerate experience.

Gaming vs. Productivity Builds

Depending on your primary use case, how you split your budget should change drastically:

  • Pure Gaming: For maximum FPS, the GPU is king. Depending on your overall budget, roughly 40-50% of your total spend should go directly to your graphics card. Games rely heavily on GPU compute and VRAM.
  • Gaming + Streaming: Streaming requires additional CPU overhead and encoding capabilities. A stronger CPU with more cores, or a GPU with a dedicated high-quality encoder (like NVIDIA's NVENC), becomes necessary, slightly shifting the balance.
  • Productivity & Editing: Applications like Premiere Pro, Blender, or heavy code compilation thrive on CPU cores, ample RAM, and fast storage. For these builds, CPU and RAM allocation increases significantly compared to pure gaming setups.

Don't Skimp on the Power Supply

A common mistake for first-time builders is putting all their budget into the CPU and GPU, and buying the cheapest power supply (PSU) available. The PSU is the heart of your system. A low-quality unit can fail and take your expensive components with it. Always allocate at least 5-8% of your budget for a reliable, well-reviewed 80+ Bronze or Gold rated unit from a reputable brand.
