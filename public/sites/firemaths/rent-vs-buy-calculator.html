Rent vs Buy Calculator: The True Cost of Homeownership

Deciding whether to buy a house or continue renting is one of the biggest financial decisions you will make. While conventional wisdom often claims that "renting is throwing money away," the reality is far more complex. This rent vs buy calculator helps you compare the long-term financial outcomes of both choices.

By factoring in the opportunity cost of your down payment, property taxes, maintenance, and home appreciation, you can discover your actual break-even horizon. If you plan to move before that break-even year, renting is likely the better financial choice.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I rent or buy?

Whether you should rent or buy depends on how long you plan to stay in the home, local market conditions, and your personal finances. Generally, if you plan to stay less than 5 years, renting is better due to the high transaction costs of buying and selling real estate. Our rent vs buy calculator clearly illustrates how your timeline affects the financial outcome.

Is it cheaper to rent or buy?

It is often cheaper to rent in the short term, but cheaper to buy in the long term. Renting offers lower upfront costs and zeroes out maintenance risk, allowing you to invest the difference in the stock market. Over decades, however, buying generally builds more wealth through property appreciation and forced equity savings.

How does a rent vs buy calculator work?

A rent vs buy calculator compares the total unrecoverable costs of renting (monthly rent and renter's insurance) against the unrecoverable costs of homeownership (mortgage interest, property taxes, maintenance, HOA, insurance) minus home appreciation. It fundamentally compares the net worth generated by home equity versus the net worth generated by investing a down payment in the market.

What are the unrecoverable costs of buying?

When buying a house, not all your money goes to building wealth. The unrecoverable costs—money that disappears forever just like rent—include property taxes, homeowners insurance, HOA fees, home maintenance (typically 1-2% of the home's value annually), closing costs, and the interest portion of your mortgage payments.

Why is opportunity cost important in rent vs buy decisions?

If you rent, you can take the money you would have used for a down payment and invest it in index funds or the stock market. The return on that investment is your opportunity cost. If the stock market outperforms your home's appreciation, renting might actually make you wealthier in the long run.
