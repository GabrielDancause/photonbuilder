What is a High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA)?

A High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA) is a type of deposit account that typically pays a significantly higher interest rate than a traditional savings account. Online banks are usually able to offer these higher rates because they have lower overhead costs compared to brick-and-mortar banks.

HYSAs are an essential tool for personal finance, especially for storing your emergency fund, saving for a down payment, or holding cash you plan to need in the short term. They offer the perfect balance of earning a return while keeping your money liquid and safe.

How to Choose the Best HYSA

When comparing high-yield savings accounts, consider the following factors:

  • Annual Percentage Yield (APY): This is the most critical metric. The higher the APY, the more your money will grow over time through compound interest. Rates are variable and can change with the Federal Reserve's target rate.
  • Fees and Minimums: Look for accounts with no monthly maintenance fees and low or zero minimum balance requirements. Fees will quickly eat into any interest you earn.
  • FDIC Insurance: Ensure the bank is a Member FDIC (or uses partner banks that are). This protects your deposits up to $250,000 per depositor, per institution, in the event of a bank failure.
  • Access and Features: Consider how quickly you can transfer money to your primary checking account. Some accounts offer "buckets" or sub-accounts to help you save for specific goals, which can be highly motivating.

Cash Management Accounts vs. Traditional HYSAs

You might notice some options on our list (like Wealthfront and Betterment) are labeled as "Cash Management Accounts" rather than traditional bank accounts. These are typically offered by non-bank financial institutions (like brokerages or robo-advisors).

They work similarly to an HYSA and often offer higher APYs and massively expanded FDIC coverage by sweeping your deposits into multiple partner banks behind the scenes. However, they may not offer the same exact features as a traditional bank account, so it's important to understand the structure before depositing funds.
