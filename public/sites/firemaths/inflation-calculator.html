Understanding the Inflation Calculator

Inflation is the silent thief of wealth. It represents the rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services rises, eroding purchasing power. As inflation occurs, every dollar you own buys a smaller percentage of a good or service. This means your money's value decreases over time, requiring you to spend more dollars tomorrow to purchase what you could buy with fewer dollars today.

This purchasing power calculator uses historical Consumer Price Index (CPI-U) data provided by the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS). By comparing the CPI of a past year to the CPI of a more recent year, we can accurately determine how much buying power a specific amount of money has gained or lost.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does an inflation calculator work?

An inflation calculator works by comparing the Consumer Price Index (CPI) of two different years. It takes an initial dollar amount, divides the CPI of the target year by the CPI of the starting year to get an inflation multiplier, and then multiplies the initial amount by that factor to determine its equivalent purchasing power.

What is $100 worth today compared to the past?

The value of $100 today depends heavily on the starting year due to compounding inflation. For example, using our tool to see what $100 in 1970 is worth today reveals a massive drop in purchasing power, meaning goods that cost $100 then would cost hundreds of dollars more now.

What is a purchasing power calculator?

A purchasing power calculator is a tool that measures how the value of money changes over time. It shows you how many goods or services a specific amount of money could buy in a past year compared to a more recent year, illustrating the erosion of cash value due to inflation.

What is the average inflation rate by decade?

Inflation varies wildly by decade. The 1970s saw massive inflation averaging over 7% annually. The 1980s averaged around 5%. The 1990s and 2000s were calmer, averaging roughly 3% and 2.5% respectively. The 2010s were historically low at under 2%, while the early 2020s saw a spike back up above 4%.

Why is it important to calculate inflation for retirement?

If you don't calculate inflation, you will drastically underestimate your retirement needs. If your expenses are $50,000 today, a 3% average inflation rate means you will need roughly $90,000 to maintain the exact same lifestyle in 20 years. Your investments must outpace inflation to preserve your real wealth.
