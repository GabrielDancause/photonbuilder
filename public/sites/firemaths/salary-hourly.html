Salary vs. Hourly: What's the Difference?

Understanding the difference between being a salaried employee and an hourly worker is crucial for financial planning. A salaried employee generally receives a fixed amount of money per year, regardless of the exact number of hours worked. An hourly employee is paid a specific rate for every hour they work.

When comparing job offers, converting an annual salary to an hourly wage (or vice versa) helps you evaluate the true value of your time. For example, a $80,000 salary might seem great, but if you're working 60 hours a week, your equivalent hourly rate is much lower than someone making $70,000 but only working 40 hours.

How Paid Time Off (PTO) Affects Your Real Hourly Wage

Many standard salary calculators divide your annual pay by 2,080 hours (52 weeks × 40 hours). However, this assumes you work every single weekday of the year, including holidays and vacation time.

If you get Paid Time Off (PTO), including vacation days, sick leave, and paid holidays, you are actually working fewer hours for the same annual salary. By subtracting your PTO hours from the standard 2,080 hours, you can calculate your Real Hourly Wage—the actual amount you earn for every hour you physically work. The more PTO you have, the higher your real hourly wage becomes.

Standard Work Hour Assumptions

When calculating pay conversions, certain standard assumptions are typically used:

  • Work Weeks: There are 52 weeks in a standard year.
  • Work Days: A typical full-time job consists of 5 days per week (260 work days per year).
  • Work Hours: Full-time is generally considered 40 hours per week (2,080 hours per year).
  • Months: Monthly pay is calculated by dividing annual salary by 12, not by multiplying weekly pay by 4 (since most months have more than 28 days).
  • Bi-weekly: Being paid every two weeks results in 26 paychecks per year.
