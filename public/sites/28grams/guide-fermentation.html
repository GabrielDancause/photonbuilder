🥬 Sauerkraut & Lacto-Fermentation

▼

The simplest introduction to fermentation. Lactic acid bacteria (LAB) consume natural sugars in vegetables to create lactic acid, preserving the food.

Parameter Value / Details
Salt Ratio 2% - 2.5% of total vegetable weight
Salt Type Sea salt, Kosher salt (No iodine or anti-caking agents)
Temperature 65°F - 72°F (18°C - 22°C)
Time 1 - 4 weeks (taste after 1 week)
Submersion Vegetables MUST stay completely submerged in brine
Quick Recipe Formula
1. Weigh cabbage (e.g., 1000g)
2. Calculate salt: 1000g * 0.02 = 20g salt
3. Shred cabbage, massage with salt until juicy (5-10 mins)
4. Pack tightly into jar, pressing down to release brine
5. Weigh down (glass weight or ziplock bag of water)
6. Cover loosely or use airlock. Wait 1-4 weeks.

🌶️ Kimchi

▼

A complex, spicy, and savory lacto-ferment. Requires a preliminary salting phase before applying the flavor paste.

Phase 1: Salting (Brining)
Soak Napa cabbage in a 5-10% brine for 4-8 hours until pliable. Rinse thoroughly and squeeze out excess water before mixing with paste.
Phase 2: The Paste
Gochugaru (Korean chili flakes), garlic, ginger, fish sauce (or soy sauce for vegan), daikon radish, green onions, and sometimes a rice flour porridge binder.
Fermentation Timeline
Leave at room temp (68°F) for 1-3 days until bubbly and slightly sour, then move to fridge. It continues to slowly ferment for months.

🍄 Kombucha

▼

A fermented, slightly alcoholic, lightly effervescent, sweetened black or green tea drink powered by a SCOBY (Symbiotic Culture Of Bacteria and Yeast).

Ingredient Ratio per 1 Gallon (3.8L) Water
Tea 6-8 bags (or 2 tbsp loose leaf black/green)
Sugar 1 cup (200g) plain white cane sugar
Starter Liquid 2 cups (470ml) strong, unflavored kombucha
SCOBY 1 pellicle (optional, starter liquid is more important)
The Two-Step Process
F1 (First Fermentation):
- Brew sweet tea, cool to room temp (< 85°F).
- Add starter liquid + SCOBY.
- Cover with breathable cloth. Ferment 7-14 days.

F2 (Second Fermentation - Carbonation):
- Bottle liquid (leaving SCOBY behind).
- Add fruit/sugar (approx 1 tbsp per 16oz).
- Seal in pressure-safe flip-top bottles.
- Wait 2-4 days at room temp, then refrigerate.

🥛 Yogurt

▼

A thermophilic (heat-loving) fermentation. Requires holding milk at a specific warm temperature to allow the yogurt cultures to multiply and set the proteins.

Step Target Temperature Purpose
1. Scald Milk 180°F (82°C) Denatures whey proteins for thicker yogurt, kills bad bacteria.
2. Cool 110°F - 115°F (43°C - 46°C) Safe temp to add culture; too hot will kill the bacteria.
3. Inoculate 110°F Whisk in 2 tbsp plain yogurt (with active cultures) per quart of milk.
4. Incubate 100°F - 110°F (38°C - 43°C) Hold for 6-12 hours in oven, Instant Pot, or cooler.

🚨 Troubleshooting & Safety

▼

Fermentation is inherently safe when basic rules are followed. The acidic environment prevents dangerous pathogens (like botulism) from surviving.

Safe: Cloudy Brine Safe: Kahm Yeast (White, filmy) Safe: Bubbling/Hissing Unsafe: Fuzzy Mold (Green/Black/Pink) Unsafe: Putrid smell
Kahm Yeast vs Mold
Kahm yeast is a white, wrinkly, powdery film on the surface. It's safe but can affect taste; skim it off. Mold is fuzzy, raised, and often colored (green, blue, pink, black). If it's fuzzy, throw the whole batch away.
Vegetables floating?
Anything exposed to air will mold. Use glass fermentation weights, a smaller jar inserted into the neck, or a food-safe bag filled with brine to keep everything submerged.
Exploding Bottles (Kombucha)
During F2, use only heavy-duty, pressure-rated flip-top bottles (like Grolsch bottles). Square decorative bottles will explode. "Burp" bottles daily to release excess pressure if unsure.

The Golden Rule of Fermentation

"Under the brine, everything is fine."

For vegetable ferments, the most crucial step is ensuring your ingredients are completely deprived of oxygen. Lactic acid bacteria are anaerobic, meaning they thrive without oxygen. Molds and bad bacteria require oxygen to survive. By keeping your vegetables submerged in a salty brine, you create a selective environment where only the good bacteria can grow.

Essential Equipment

  • Digital Kitchen Scale: Essential for calculating precise salt percentages. Volumetric measurements (teaspoons/tablespoons) for salt are dangerously inaccurate because grain sizes vary wildly.
  • Glass Jars: Mason jars (wide-mouth) are the standard. Avoid metal containers (except stainless steel) as acid will react with them.
  • Fermentation Weights: Heavy glass discs designed to hold vegetables below the brine. A cheap alternative is a ziplock bag filled with brine (use brine in the bag so if it leaks, it doesn't dilute your ferment).
  • Airlocks (Optional but recommended): Silicone nipple lids or water airlocks allow CO2 to escape without letting oxygen or fruit flies in.

Why Ferment at Home?

Store-bought pickles and sauerkraut are often pasteurized and canned using vinegar. This process kills all the beneficial probiotic bacteria. Home fermentation relies on natural bacterial processes, resulting in food rich in living probiotics, complex flavors, and enhanced nutrient bioavailability.
