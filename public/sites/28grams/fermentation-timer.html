The Science of Fermentation & Temperature

Fermentation is a biological process driven by microorganisms like yeast and bacteria. Because these are living cultures, their activity rate is highly dependent on their environment—most notably, the ambient temperature.

  • The Q10 Temperature Coefficient: In biology, the Q10 value represents the factor by which the rate of a reaction increases for every 10°C rise in temperature. Most sourdough and yeast ferments have a Q10 around 2.0. This means if your kitchen is 10°C (18°F) warmer, your bread will proof twice as fast.
  • Sourdough Bulk Fermentation: A standard sourdough recipe assumes a room temperature of 21-22°C (70-72°F). If your kitchen is hot (e.g., 28°C / 82°F) in the summer, a bulk fermentation that usually takes 5 hours could be done in less than 3 hours.
  • Yogurt & Thermophilic Cultures: Yogurt bacteria thrive at much higher temperatures (~42°C / 108°F). Fermenting at lower temperatures will result in a much slower set and often a thinner, sweeter yogurt.

How to Use This Tool

This timer uses biological models to approximate how long your ferment will take based on the temperature of the room or fermentation chamber you are using.

  • Step 1: Select your target ferment from the dropdown list.
  • Step 2: Enter the ambient temperature where the vessel will be sitting. Use the toggle to switch between Celsius and Fahrenheit.
  • Step 3: (Optional) Adjust your inoculation percentage if you are using more or less starter than a standard recipe calls for. Note: This has the biggest impact on sourdough.
  • Step 4: Input the time you plan to start, and copy the resulting timeline to your notes or calendar!

Note: Sourdough and other ferments are living things. Always watch the dough/culture, not the clock. This calculator provides a highly accurate estimate, but visual cues are always the final test.
