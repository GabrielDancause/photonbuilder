Day 2-3: The False Rise

discardfeed1:1:1

You may see a massive burst of activity and a bad smell. This is a bacterial bloom (leuconostoc bacteria), not yeast. Do not bake with this. Keep feeding it to lower the pH.

Daily Feed (1:1:1 Ratio) ------------------------ 1. Discard down to 50g starter 2. Add 50g Water (room temp) 3. Add 50g Flour (50/50 AP/Whole Wheat) 4. Mix well, cover loosely. Rest 24h.

Day 4-5: The Quiet Phase

Patience
waitingacidic

Activity will likely stop completely. It is not dead. The environment is becoming acidic enough to kill off unwanted bacteria, making room for wild yeast to wake up.

Action: Continue discarding and feeding at a 1:1:1 ratio once a day. Keep it warm (70-75°F / 21-24°C).

Day 6-7+: Predictable Rise

Ready
readydoubling

The starter should now predictably double in size within 4-8 hours after feeding and smell pleasantly sour/yeasty. Once it doubles consistently for 3 days in a row, it's ready to bake.

Action: You can now switch to a maintenance schedule.

⚖️ Maintenance & Feeding

Counter Maintenance (Baking Daily)

If you bake often, keep it on the counter. The feeding ratio determines how long it takes to peak.

Ratios & Timing (approximate at 72°F) -------------------------------------- 1:1:1 (e.g. 20g S / 20g W / 20g F) -> Peaks in 3-5 hrs 1:2:2 (e.g. 10g S / 20g W / 20g F) -> Peaks in 6-8 hrs 1:5:5 (e.g. 5g S / 25g W / 25g F) -> Peaks in 10-12 hrs (Overnight)

Fridge Maintenance (Baking Weekly)

If you bake once a week or less, store it in the fridge to slow fermentation and save flour.

  1. Feed starter at a 1:2:2 ratio.
  2. Leave on counter for 1-2 hours until you see 20% growth.
  3. Put a tight lid on and place in the fridge.
  4. To bake: Take it out, feed it once or twice on the counter until it doubles, then use.

🚑 Troubleshooting

Diagnosis: Your starter is very hungry.

Fix: The liquid is harmless alcohol produced by yeast. You can pour it off or stir it in. Discard most of the starter and feed it a larger ratio (like 1:3:3 or 1:5:5) to give the yeast more food.

Diagnosis: It's too cold, or the yeast population is weak.

Fix: Move it to a warmer spot (e.g., inside the oven with just the light on, or on top of the fridge). Switch to using 50% Whole Wheat or Rye flour in your feedings to boost nutrients.

Diagnosis: Contamination.

Fix: THROW IT AWAY. Do not try to save it. Wash your jar thoroughly with hot soapy water, sanitize it, and start over.

The Science of a Sourdough Starter

A sourdough starter is a symbiotic culture of bacteria and yeast (SCOBY). The wild yeast (primarily Saccharomyces cerevisiae and Candida milleri) is responsible for producing carbon dioxide, which makes your bread rise. The lactic acid bacteria (LAB) produce lactic and acetic acids, which give the bread its signature sour flavor, lower the pH to prevent mold, and break down gluten to make the bread more digestible.

Why Discard?

Many beginners hate throwing away flour. However, discarding before feeding is mathematically necessary. If you don't discard, you must feed an exponentially growing amount of flour to maintain the proper food-to-microbe ratio. If you keep 100g of starter, you must feed it 100g of flour. The next day, you have 300g of starter, which requires 300g of flour, and so on. Discarding keeps the culture at a manageable size and prevents the acidity levels from becoming too toxic for the yeast to survive.

Understanding Hydration in Starters

This guide assumes a 100% hydration starter, which is the standard for most modern recipes. This simply means you feed it equal parts by weight of water and flour. Because water is denser than flour, a 100% hydration starter will look like thick pancake batter. If you use volume measurements (like cups), equal parts water and flour will result in a hydration closer to 200%, which is too watery and will struggle to trap gas bubbles.
