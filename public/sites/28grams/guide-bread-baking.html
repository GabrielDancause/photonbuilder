Bread Baking Guide 🥖

Your cheat sheet for getting started with baking bread at home. Equipment, flour, techniques, and your first recipe.

🔍

🛠️ Essential Equipment

▼

Digital Scale

The most important tool. Measure in grams for precision and consistency. Volumes (cups) vary wildly for flour.

Bench Scraper

Indispensable for moving, dividing, and shaping dough without sticking to your hands. Also great for cleaning counters.

Instant-Read Thermometer

Water temp controls fermentation time. Bread is fully baked when the internal temp hits 190°F-205°F (88°C-96°C).

Dutch Oven

Creates a sealed, steamy environment in home ovens. Essential for achieving a thick, crispy artisan crust.

🌾 Common Flour Types

▼
Flour Type Protein % Best Used For
All-Purpose (AP) 10 - 12% Soft rolls, flatbreads, everyday simple loaves. Lower water absorption.
Bread Flour 12 - 14% Chewy artisan breads, bagels, sourdough. High gluten forms strong structure.
Whole Wheat 13 - 15% Hearty breads. Absorbs a lot of water. Sharp bran edges can cut gluten strands.
Rye Flour Varies Dense, flavorful breads. Very low in gluten-forming proteins. Sticky to handle.

👐 Handling Techniques

▼

Autolyse

Mixing just flour and water and letting it sit (20 mins to 2 hours) before adding salt/yeast. Fully hydrates flour and kickstarts gluten development automatically.

Kneading

Mechanically developing gluten by repeatedly folding and pushing the dough. Good for stiff, low-hydration doughs (like bagels or sandwich bread).

Stretch & Fold

Used for wet (high hydration) doughs. Grab one side of the dough, stretch it up, and fold it over itself. Repeat on all 4 sides. Usually done 3-4 times every 30 mins.

Shaping

Creating surface tension on the outside of the dough before baking. This tension gives the bread vertical "oven spring" instead of spreading flat into a pancake.

⏱️ The Fermentation Stages

▼
Stage Description What to look for
Bulk Fermentation
(First Rise)		 The entire mass of dough ferments together after mixing. Flavor develops here. Dough should increase in volume (50-100%), feel airy, and have a smooth, domed top.
Dividing & Preshape Cutting the dough into individual loaf sizes and gently rounding them. Let sit for 10-20 mins (bench rest) to relax the gluten before final shaping.
Final Proof
(Second Rise)		 Dough is shaped and placed in a basket (banneton) or pan for its final rise. The Poke Test: Poke the dough. If it springs back slowly and leaves a slight indent, it's ready to bake.

📝 Your First Recipe: The Saturday Loaf

▼

A very forgiving, straight-dough recipe using instant yeast. Takes about 4-5 hours from start to finish. Yields one standard loaf. (72% hydration). 

INGREDIENTS:
- 500g Bread Flour (or All-Purpose)
- 360g Warm Water (approx. 90°F / 32°C)
- 10g Fine Sea Salt
- 3g Instant Yeast (approx. 1 scant tsp)

INSTRUCTIONS:
1. Mix all ingredients in a large bowl until a shaggy dough forms.
2. Cover and let rest for 20 minutes.
3. Perform 3 sets of "Stretch and Folds", waiting 30 mins between each.
4. Let the dough rise covered until doubled in size (about 1.5 to 2 hours depending on room temp).
5. Gently turn dough out onto a lightly floured surface. Shape into a round ball, building tension on the top.
6. Place upside down into a floured proofing basket (or a bowl lined with a floured towel).
7. Final proof for 45-60 mins. Preheat oven (with Dutch oven inside) to 475°F (245°C).
8. Flip dough onto parchment paper. Score the top with a razor or sharp knife.
9. Carefully transfer to hot Dutch oven. Bake with lid on for 30 mins.
10. Remove lid, reduce heat to 450°F (230°C), bake 10-15 more mins until deep golden brown.
11. Cool completely (at least 2 hours) on a wire rack before slicing!

Why Bake Your Own Bread?

Baking bread at home transforms simple, inexpensive ingredients—flour, water, salt, and yeast—into something extraordinary. Beyond the incredible smell that fills your house, homemade bread is free of the commercial preservatives, dough conditioners, and excessive sugars found in supermarket loaves. You have total control over what goes into your body.

The Role of Time and Temperature

In bread baking, temperature is an ingredient. Yeast (whether commercial or wild sourdough) acts faster in warm environments and slower in cold ones. Professional bakers manipulate temperature to control time.

  • Warm ferments: Fast rising times, milder flavor. Good for quick yeast breads.
  • Cold ferments (The fridge): Slow rising times, complex flavor development, and better crust blistering. Often called "retarding" the dough.

If your dough is rising too fast, you can put it in the fridge to slow it down. If it's too slow, find a warmer spot in your kitchen.

Mastering Baker's Math

As you progress, you'll start seeing recipes written in percentages rather than grams or cups. This is called Baker's Percentage. In this system, the total flour weight is always 100%. Every other ingredient is a percentage of that flour weight. This makes scaling recipes up or down incredibly easy.
