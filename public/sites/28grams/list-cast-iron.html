🧼 Care Guides

Washing: Yes, you can use dish soap! Modern dish soap does not contain lye and will not strip your seasoning. Wash with warm water and a sponge or chainmail scrubber.

Drying: Never let it air dry. Dry thoroughly with a towel, or place it on the stove over low heat for a few minutes until completely bone dry.

Maintenance: After drying, while the pan is still slightly warm, rub a microscopic layer of neutral oil (like grapeseed or canola) all over the inside and wipe it out until it looks dry again. Store in a dry place.

Avoid: Never put in the dishwasher. Do not soak in the sink. Avoid cooking highly acidic foods (like heavy tomato sauces) for long periods until the seasoning is very well established.

Washing: Wash with warm soapy water and a non-abrasive sponge. While technically dishwasher safe, hand washing is highly recommended to preserve the enamel's finish over time.

Stains & Stuck Food: If food is stuck, simmer some water and baking soda in the pan for 10-15 minutes, then let it cool before scrubbing. Barkeepers Friend (the soft cleanser version) is also excellent for removing stains.

Avoid: Thermal shock. Never put a hot enameled pan into cold water, or heat a cold pan too quickly on high heat, as this can cause the glass enamel to crack or pop off. Never use metal utensils, which can scratch the enamel; use wood or silicone.

Why Choose Cast Iron?

Cast iron skillets are prized for their unparalleled heat retention and durability. While they take longer to heat up than aluminum or stainless steel, once hot, they stay hot. This makes them the absolute best tool for searing steaks, achieving a crispy crust on cornbread, or shallow frying chicken. A well-maintained raw cast iron pan also develops a naturally non-stick patina over time, reducing the need for synthetic coatings like Teflon.

Raw vs. Enameled Cast Iron

Raw Cast Iron (Lodge, Field): These pans are a single piece of cast iron. They require seasoning—a process where oil is baked onto the iron to create a protective, non-stick polymer layer. They are virtually indestructible and can be used over campfires, on grills, and in the oven at any temperature. However, they require specific care (no soaking, immediate drying) to prevent rust.

Enameled Cast Iron (Le Creuset, Staub): These feature a raw cast iron core completely covered in a glass particulate called enamel. The enamel protects the iron from rust and eliminates the need for seasoning. It also makes the pan non-reactive, meaning you can slowly simmer acidic tomato sauces or wine-based braises without worrying about stripping seasoning or imparting a metallic taste to your food. The trade-off is that enamel is a glass product; it can chip if dropped or subjected to extreme temperature changes (thermal shock).
