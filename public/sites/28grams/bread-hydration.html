What is Bread Hydration?

Hydration is one of the most important concepts in bread baking. It refers to the ratio of water (or total liquid) to total flour in your recipe, expressed as a percentage using Baker's Math. In this system, the total flour weight is always exactly 100%.

For example, if a recipe calls for 1000g of flour and 700g of water, the hydration is 70% (700 ÷ 1000 = 0.70).

How Hydration Affects Your Bread

  • Crumb Structure: Higher hydration generally leads to a more "open" crumb (larger, irregular holes), while lower hydration yields a tighter, more uniform crumb (great for sandwiches).
  • Handling & Shaping: Lower hydration doughs are stiff, less sticky, and easier to knead and shape. High hydration doughs are very wet, sticky, and often require techniques like "stretch and fold" or coil folds instead of traditional kneading.
  • Crust & Volume: Wetter doughs create more steam inside the loaf as they bake, which can contribute to greater "oven spring" (rise) and a thinner, crispier crust.

A Guide to Hydration Levels

  • Low Hydration (< 60%): Very stiff dough. Examples include bagels, pretzels, and some stiff sourdough starters. Easy to handle.
  • Standard Hydration (60% - 70%): The sweet spot for many traditional breads, including sandwich loaves and beginner sourdough. A good balance of flavor, manageable texture, and a soft, even crumb.
  • High Hydration (70% - 85%): Typical for ciabatta and advanced sourdough boules. Wet, sticky, and challenging to handle, but yields a dramatic, open crumb with big holes and a light, airy texture.
  • Extreme Hydration (85%+): Very challenging, almost batter-like consistency. Used for specialty breads like pan de cristal. Requires significant experience and specialized flours with high protein content to build enough strength.
