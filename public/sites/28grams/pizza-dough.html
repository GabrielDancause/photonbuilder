Pizza Dough Styles Explained

  
Different pizza styles require entirely different dough compositions. The percentages below represent standard Baker's Percentages, where flour is always exactly 100%, and everything else is relative to the flour weight.

  
    
    
  • Neapolitan: The classic. A very simple dough requiring extreme heat (800°F+). Usually made with fine '00' flour, around 60-65% hydration, and absolutely no oil or sugar.
    • 
    
  • New York: Designed for slightly lower temperatures (500°F - 600°F) in standard deck ovens. Often uses Bread Flour, 60-65% hydration, and incorporates a bit of sugar (for browning) and oil (for tenderness).
    • 
    
  • Detroit: A high-hydration (70-75%) pan pizza. The dough is very wet and airy, baked in a steel pan generously coated with oil or shortening to fry the bottom crust.
    • 
  


  
Understanding Hydration

  
Hydration is the ratio of water to flour. A higher hydration dough (like Detroit style) will be much stickier to handle but will result in a more open, airy crumb. A lower hydration dough (like NY style) is firmer, easier to toss, and produces a denser, chewier crust that can support heavy toppings.

  
If you're using standard all-purpose flour instead of bread flour or '00' flour, you may want to lower your hydration by 2-3%, as all-purpose flour absorbs less water.
