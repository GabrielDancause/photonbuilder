How to Calculate Smoking Time

Smoking meat is an art that depends heavily on temperature control and time. A general rule of thumb for large cuts like brisket or pork shoulder is 1 to 1.5 hours per pound at 225°F to 250°F. However, this is just an estimate. The actual time depends on the thickness of the meat, humidity, smoker airflow, and how often you open the lid.

The "Stall"

When smoking large cuts like brisket or pork butt, you will likely encounter "the stall" — a period where the internal temperature stops rising, usually around 150°F to 165°F (65°C to 74°C). This happens because of evaporative cooling as the meat sweats out moisture. You can wait it out, or you can wrap the meat tightly in butcher paper or foil (the "Texas Crutch") to push through the stall faster.

Target Temperatures & Resting

  • Brisket & Pork Butt: We aim for an internal temperature of about 200°F - 205°F (93°C - 96°C). The meat should be probe-tender (like pushing a probe into warm butter). Resting for at least 1-2 hours is critical for the juices to redistribute.
  • Poultry (Chicken/Turkey): Needs to reach a safe internal temperature of 165°F (74°C) in the thickest part of the breast and thigh. Smoking poultry at higher temps (275°F - 325°F) helps render the fat and crisp the skin.
  • Pork Ribs: Instead of checking internal temperature, rely on the "bend test". When you pick up the rack with tongs in the middle, it should bend and the bark should start to crack.
