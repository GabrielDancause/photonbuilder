The Importance of the Grind

If you have to choose between upgrading your coffee machine or your grinder, always choose the grinder. The best espresso machine in the world cannot fix a poor grind. Inconsistent particle sizes lead to channeling, bitter over-extraction, and sour under-extraction simultaneously.

A good burr grinder ensures uniform particle size, which is the foundation of a balanced, repeatable cup of coffee—whether you're brewing pour-over, French press, or espresso.

Flat vs. Conical Burrs: What's the Difference?

Burr geometry plays a significant role in the flavor profile of your coffee:

  • Conical Burrs: Generally produce a "bimodal" grind distribution (two distinct particle sizes). This results in a thicker body, complex texture, and traditionally sweet, chocolatey espresso profiles. They are often quieter and retain less coffee.
  • Flat Burrs: Produce a highly "unimodal" grind (very uniform particle size). This emphasizes clarity, flavor separation, and high acidity. Flat burrs are highly prized for light-roast filter coffee and modern, high-extraction espresso.

Manual vs. Electric

Should you buy a hand grinder or an electric one?

  • Manual Grinders: Offer unmatched value. A $150 hand grinder can rival the grind quality of a $500 electric grinder because you aren't paying for a motor or electronics. They are portable, quiet, and retain nearly zero coffee. The downside is the physical effort—grinding for espresso manually can take 45-60 seconds of vigorous cranking.
  • Electric Grinders: Provide convenience and speed. If you make multiple coffees back-to-back or primarily drink espresso, an electric grinder is usually worth the investment. However, cheap electric burr grinders often suffer from alignment issues and high retention.
