Baking vs. Cooking Needs

Not all scales are built the same. When cooking, precise measurements aren't always critical. A margin of error of a few grams won't ruin a stew or a salad. What matters most for cooking is a decent capacity and a platform large enough to hold a mixing bowl without obscuring the display.

When baking, however, chemistry dictates the outcome. Flour volume can vary wildly depending on how it settled in the bag, which is why bakers always use weight. For bread and pastry, you want a scale that responds quickly as you pour ingredients and has a long auto-off time so it doesn't shut down in the middle of measuring.

Accuracy: 1g vs 0.1g

Most standard kitchen scales have a resolution of 1g. This is perfectly fine for measuring flour, water, butter, and sugar. However, 1g precision struggles with small but potent ingredients. If a recipe calls for 2 grams of yeast, a standard scale might jump from 0g to 3g instantly, leaving you guessing.

If you regularly bake bread, brew specialty coffee, or measure spices, a micro scale with 0.1g precision is an invaluable secondary tool. Many bakers use a high-capacity scale for bulk ingredients and a pocket scale specifically for salt, yeast, and leaveners.

Why Tare Weight Matters

The "Tare" or "Zero" function is the most used button on any scale. It allows you to place an empty bowl on the scale, hit Tare to reset the weight to zero, and then measure your ingredient directly into the bowl. This saves you from doing math or dirtying multiple measuring cups.

When comparing scales, look at the tactile feel of the Tare button. Some scales use capacitive touch buttons (like a smartphone screen), which can be frustrating if your fingers are covered in flour or dough. Physical, sealed buttons (like on the Escali Primo) are often preferred by serious bakers.
