What is a Sleep Cycle?

A normal sleep cycle lasts roughly 90 minutes. During this time, your brain moves through several stages of sleep, ranging from light sleep to deep sleep, and finally into REM (Rapid Eye Movement) sleep, where most dreaming occurs.

Waking up in the middle of a deep sleep cycle often leaves you feeling groggy, disoriented, and exhausted—a state known as sleep inertia. By calculating your sleep in 90-minute intervals, you can aim to wake up at the end of a cycle, during your lightest sleep stage, which helps you feel refreshed and alert instantly.

How Many Sleep Cycles Do I Need?

Most adults need between 5 and 6 sleep cycles per night to function optimally.

  • 6 Cycles (9 hours): Ideal for athletes, young adults, or people recovering from illness or extreme fatigue.
  • 5 Cycles (7.5 hours): The sweet spot for most healthy adults, providing the optimal balance of rest.
  • 4 Cycles (6 hours): Adequate for some people, but regularly sleeping only 6 hours can lead to a long-term sleep deficit.

Why Factor in "Time to Fall Asleep"?

One of the most common mistakes people make when planning their sleep schedule is forgetting that we don't instantly fall asleep the moment our head hits the pillow. On average, it takes a healthy adult about 14 to 20 minutes to fall asleep (this is called sleep latency).

If you aim for exactly 7.5 hours of sleep but it takes you 20 minutes to drift off, you'll actually wake up right in the middle of your final sleep cycle. Factoring in your average sleep latency ensures your 90-minute cycles align perfectly with your alarm clock.
