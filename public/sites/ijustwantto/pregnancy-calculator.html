How is the Due Date Calculated?

An estimated due date (EDD) is calculated based on either the first day of your last menstrual period (LMP) or the date of conception. A typical pregnancy lasts about 280 days (40 weeks) from the first day of the last menstrual period.

If you have a standard 28-day cycle, Naegele's rule is often used: add seven days to the first day of your LMP, then subtract three months. If your cycle is longer or shorter than 28 days, the calculator adjusts the due date by adding or subtracting the difference in days.

If you know your exact conception date (for example, through IVF or tracking ovulation), the calculator simply adds 266 days (38 weeks) to that date to determine your estimated due date.

Understanding the Trimesters

Pregnancy is divided into three trimesters, each marking different stages of fetal development and changes in the mother's body:

  • First Trimester (Weeks 1-13): This is a period of rapid development. Major organs and body systems begin to form. Many women experience early pregnancy symptoms like morning sickness and fatigue during this time.
  • Second Trimester (Weeks 14-27): Often considered the most comfortable trimester. The baby continues to grow and develop features like hair and fingerprints. You may start to feel the baby move (quickening) halfway through this trimester.
  • Third Trimester (Weeks 28-40+): The final stretch of pregnancy. The baby is gaining weight rapidly and their organs are maturing in preparation for birth. You may experience more physical discomfort as the baby grows larger.

Important Milestones

Throughout your pregnancy, there are several key developmental milestones and medical checkpoints:

  • 6-8 Weeks: A fetal heartbeat can usually be detected via ultrasound.
  • 18-22 Weeks: The mid-pregnancy anatomy scan is typically performed to check the baby's development and determine the sex if desired.
  • 24 Weeks: This is generally considered the point of viability, meaning a baby born at this stage has a chance of survival with intensive medical care.
  • 37-40 Weeks: A baby is considered "full term" at 37-39 weeks, arriving anytime between 37 and 42 weeks is normal.
