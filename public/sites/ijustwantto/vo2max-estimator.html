What is VO2 Max?

VO2 max, or maximal oxygen consumption, refers to the maximum amount of oxygen that an individual can utilize during intense or maximal exercise. It is measured in milliliters of oxygen used in one minute per kilogram of body weight (mL/kg/min). It is widely considered the best indicator of cardiovascular fitness and aerobic endurance.

A higher VO2 max means your body can take in more oxygen and deliver it to your muscles, enabling you to run faster, cycle longer, and generally perform aerobic activities more efficiently without fatiguing.

How the Estimations Work

Testing your true VO2 max requires a specialized laboratory setup with a treadmill or cycle ergometer and a mask to measure your oxygen consumption directly. Because this is expensive and inaccessible for most people, several reliable field tests have been developed to estimate VO2 max:

  • Cooper Test (12-Minute Run): Developed by Dr. Kenneth Cooper in 1968 for the US military. The goal is to cover as much distance as possible in 12 minutes. The test has a high correlation with lab-tested VO2 max.
  • Rockport 1-Mile Walk Test: Ideal for individuals who are not runners. It estimates your cardiovascular fitness based on how fast you can walk a mile and what your heart rate is immediately upon finishing.
  • Resting Heart Rate Method: A quick estimation based on the ratio of your maximum heart rate to your resting heart rate. While convenient, it is generally less precise than performance-based field tests.

Improving Your VO2 Max

To improve your cardiorespiratory fitness, you should incorporate both steady-state aerobic training (Zone 2) and High-Intensity Interval Training (HIIT). Zone 2 training builds your aerobic base, while HIIT pushes your cardiovascular system near its limit to increase your maximum capacity.
