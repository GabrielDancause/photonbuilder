Complete Guide to Freelancing as a Designer in Quebec

From registering your business to finding clients and navigating Quebec's unique legal and financial landscape. A comprehensive resource for designers taking the independent route.

Updated for 2026 • 15 min read

Transitioning into freelance design in Quebec is a rewarding career move, offering autonomy, higher earning potential, and the freedom to choose your projects. However, the province has a distinct legal, financial, and regulatory framework that you must navigate correctly to ensure your business thrives. Whether you are transitioning from a full-time agency role or starting fresh out of school, this guide covers absolutely everything a graphic, UX/UI, or product designer needs to know to establish and run a successful freelance practice in Quebec.

This resource acts as your roadmap. We will cover the crucial differences between sole proprietorship and incorporation, how to manage your sales taxes (TPS/TVQ), client acquisition strategies in the local market, and essential tools that will save you hundreds of hours. Additionally, we've included interactive calculators specifically tailored for Quebec freelancers to help you price your services and calculate your tax obligations.

⚖️ Legal Structure & Registration

Before you send your first invoice, you need to establish a legal framework for your business. In Quebec, freelance designers typically choose between two main structures: Sole Proprietorship (Travailleur Autonome) and Incorporation (Société par actions).

Sole Proprietorship vs. Incorporation

Sole Proprietorship (Travailleur Autonome): This is the simplest and most common way to start. You and your business are considered the same legal entity. This means you report your business income on your personal tax return (TP1 in Quebec, T1 federally). It is incredibly easy to set up and costs almost nothing.

Incorporation: An incorporated business is a separate legal entity. It offers limited liability (protecting your personal assets if your business is sued) and potential tax advantages if you earn significantly more than you need to live on (usually recommended when netting over $80,000 - $100,000+ CAD annually). However, it involves much higher accounting and legal fees, separate corporate tax returns, and more rigorous bookkeeping.

Verdict for new designers: Start as a Sole Proprietor. You can always incorporate later when your revenue justifies the administrative costs.

Registering with the NEQ (Numéro d'entreprise du Québec)

If you are a sole proprietor and you operate under your own legal name (e.g., "Jane Doe"), you are not required to register with the Registraire des entreprises du Québec (REQ). However, if you add any descriptive words (e.g., "Jane Doe Design", "JD Creative", or "Studio Lumina"), you must register to obtain a Numéro d'entreprise du Québec (NEQ). Registration costs around $39 CAD annually and takes just a few minutes online via the Quebec government portal.

Sales Taxes: TPS & TVQ Numbers

In Canada, the Goods and Services Tax (GST/TPS) is 5%, and the Quebec Sales Tax (QST/TVQ) is 9.975%. Combined, you will be charging 14.975% on your invoices to Quebec clients.

When do you need to register? You are legally required to register for TPS/TVQ as soon as your worldwide gross revenues from taxable sales exceed $30,000 CAD in a single calendar quarter or over four consecutive calendar quarters. If you make less than this, you are considered a "small supplier" and do not have to charge sales tax (though you cannot claim input tax credits for the taxes you pay on expenses, either).

Even if you are below $30,000, many designers choose to register voluntarily immediately. It makes you look more professional to corporate clients, and it allows you to claim back the taxes you pay on business expenses (like your Adobe Creative Cloud subscription or a new MacBook).

Professional Liability Insurance

Errors and Omissions (E&O) insurance is highly recommended. If you design a billboard with a typo that goes to print, or if a website you designed crashes during a major launch, the client could hold you liable for lost revenue. Professional liability insurance (often bundled with commercial general liability) protects you. Providers like Zensurance or local Quebec brokers can offer tailored packages for graphic designers for around $300-$600 CAD per year.

💰 Financial Management & Taxes

Managing your money correctly from day one is the difference between thriving and constantly stressing about tax season.

The Rule of Thirds

🚨 Best Practice

Every time a client pays you, immediately divide the money:

  • 30-35% goes into a separate savings account for Income Tax and Acomptes Provisionnels.
  • 15% (if you collected TPS/TVQ) goes into a tax account to remit to Revenu Québec later.
  • The remainder is your actual business revenue to pay expenses and yourself.

Never treat sales tax collected as your own money.

Quarterly Tax Installments (Acomptes Provisionnels)

Unlike employees who have taxes deducted from every paycheck, freelancers get paid the gross amount. If you owe more than $1,800 CAD in net tax at the end of the year in Quebec, Revenu Québec and the CRA will require you to pay taxes in quarterly installments the following year (March 15, June 15, September 15, December 15). Save for these proactively.

Deductible Business Expenses

As a freelancer, you are taxed on your net profit (Revenue minus Expenses), not your gross revenue. Keep every receipt. Common deductions for designers include:

  • Software: Adobe CC, Figma, Midjourney, Webflow hosting, Dropbox.
  • Hardware: Computers, monitors, tablets (these are depreciated as Capital Cost Allowance over several years).
  • Home Office: A percentage of your rent, electricity, and internet, based on the square footage of your dedicated workspace relative to your total home.
  • Marketing: Portfolio hosting, domain names, business cards, advertising.
  • Professional Fees: Accountant fees, legal advice, NEQ renewal.

RRSP (REER) vs. TFSA (CELI) for Freelancers

Freelancers do not have employer-matched pensions, so you must fund your own retirement. An RRSP (REER) is excellent because contributions are tax-deductible, lowering your taxable income for the year—very useful if you have a highly profitable year. A TFSA (CELI) is funded with after-tax money, but all growth and withdrawals are completely tax-free. Many freelancers max out their TFSA first to build an accessible emergency fund (crucial for dry spells in freelance work) before focusing on RRSP contributions.

🤝 Clients, Pricing & Contracts

Your design skills are only half the equation; the other half is selling those skills.

Finding Clients in Quebec

The Montreal and broader Quebec market is relationship-driven. While platforms like Upwork exist, the best-paying clients are found locally.

  • Local Agencies: Many Montreal advertising and marketing agencies use freelancers to handle overflow work or specialized tasks (like motion design or 3D). Reach out to Creative Directors directly on LinkedIn.
  • Networking: Attend local events like CreativeMornings Montreal, AIGA events, or industry-specific meetups.
  • Job Boards (Search URLs): Actively look for freelance or contract roles. For example, search LinkedIn for Quebec freelance design roles or Guichet-Emplois for pigiste opportunities.

Pricing Models

There is no "one size fits all" pricing, but you should aim to move away from hourly billing as you gain experience.

$50-$85 Junior Hourly (0-3 yrs)
$85-$125 Mid-Level Hourly (3-7 yrs)
$125-$200+ Senior/Specialist Hourly

Value-Based Pricing: Instead of charging for your time, charge for the value the project brings to the client. A logo for a local bakery might be $1,500; the exact same logo process for a funded tech startup might be $8,000, because the risk, usage, and value to the startup are significantly higher.

Contracts, Deposits, and Scope Creep

Never start work without a signed contract and a deposit. A standard practice is 50% upfront, 50% upon final delivery (before handing over the source files). For larger projects, you might do 30% upfront, 30% at a specific milestone, and 40% at the end.

Your contract must define Scope Creep. State clearly how many rounds of revisions are included (usually 2 or 3). Anything beyond that is billed at your hourly rate. This protects your profitability.

🛠️ Essential Tools for Freelancers

Running a freelance business means you wear many hats: designer, accountant, project manager, and sales rep. These tools will help you manage the load efficiently.

  • Accounting & Invoicing: Wave Accounting is completely free for invoicing and basic accounting, and supports dual tax rates (perfect for TPS/TVQ). For more robust needs, QuickBooks Online Canada is the industry standard.
  • Time Tracking: Toggl Track or Harvest. Tracking your time is essential even if you charge flat project rates, so you can measure your actual profitability.
  • Project Management: Notion or Asana to track project statuses, client feedback, and deadlines.
  • Contracts: Use templates from platforms like Bonsai or the AIGA standard form of agreement, but always adapt them to your specific needs.

⚜️ Quebec-Specific Context

Doing business in Quebec requires awareness of provincial regulations that do not exist elsewhere in Canada.

Bill 96 & Language Requirements

Under the Charter of the French Language (enhanced by Bill 96), French is the official language of commerce and business in Quebec. If you are serving Quebec clients or consumers, you must be able to serve them in French. Your contracts, invoices, and public-facing websites targeting the Quebec market should ideally be bilingual or French-first. If a Quebec-based client requests a contract in French, you are legally obligated to provide it.

CNESST Obligations

The Commission des normes, de l'équité, de la santé et de la sécurité du travail (CNESST) manages occupational health and safety. As a sole proprietor without employees, you are generally not required to register or pay CNESST premiums. However, you can opt into voluntary coverage. This ensures that if you are injured at work (e.g., a severe RSI or an accident while traveling to a client's office), you have income replacement.

Industry Associations

Joining the SDGQ (Société des designers graphiques du Québec) adds immense credibility to your profile. They offer networking events, standard contract templates tailored to Quebec law, and guidelines for ethical pricing and spec work.

✅ First 30 Days Checklist

Ready to take the plunge? Follow this structured 30-day plan to launch your freelance design career.

Days 1-7: The Foundation

  • Define your niche (e.g., UI for SaaS, branding for hospitality).
  • Choose a business name and register your NEQ if applicable.
  • Set up a dedicated business bank account (never mix personal and business funds).
  • Update your portfolio with 3-4 highly relevant, polished case studies.

Days 8-15: Legal & Financial

  • Register for TPS/TVQ numbers (if anticipating >$30k, or voluntarily).
  • Create an account with Wave Accounting or QuickBooks.
  • Draft a standard contract template outlining payment terms and revisions.
  • Determine your hourly rate and standard project minimums.

Days 16-30: Outreach & Launch

  • Update your LinkedIn profile to "Freelance Designer" and turn on "Open to Work" (contract roles).
  • Make a list of 50 local agencies or ideal clients in Montreal/Quebec.
  • Send personalized, brief emails to Creative Directors introducing yourself.
  • Announce your freelance launch to your existing network and friends.
