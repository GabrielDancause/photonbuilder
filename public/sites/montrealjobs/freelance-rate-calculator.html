The Reality of Freelance Pricing in Quebec

Pricing your freelance services correctly in Quebec isn't just about matching what others charge. It's about ensuring your survival and growth as a business. Many new freelancers make the critical error of taking their desired salary (e.g., $60,000), dividing it by 2080 hours (40 hours x 52 weeks), and arriving at $28.84/hr. This is a fast track to burnout.

Why? Because as a freelancer, you don't bill 40 hours a week. You spend time on marketing, client meetings, accounting, and quoting. A healthy target is 20-25 billable hours per week. Furthermore, you must cover your own vacation time, sick days, hardware, software subscriptions, and pay both the employer and employee portions of the Quebec Pension Plan (RRQ).

Quebec Tax Obligations: TPS/TVQ Explained

Operating as a freelancer in Quebec means navigating the rules set by Revenu Québec and the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA). The most immediate concern for new freelancers is sales tax: the Goods and Services Tax (GST/TPS) and the Quebec Sales Tax (QST/TVQ).

The $30,000 Threshold

In Quebec, you are considered a "small supplier" and are not required to register for or collect TPS and TVQ if your total worldwide taxable supplies (your gross revenue before expenses) do not exceed $30,000 in a single calendar quarter or in four consecutive calendar quarters.

However, registering voluntarily before hitting this threshold is often a smart move:

  1. Input Tax Credits (ITCs/ITRs): Once registered, you can claim back the TPS and TVQ you pay on your business expenses (like Adobe CC subscriptions, a new MacBook, or part of your internet bill).
  2. Professionalism: Charging taxes makes you look like an established business. Corporate clients expect to pay it (they just claim it back anyway) and might view a freelancer who doesn't charge tax as a hobbyist.

If you cross the $30,000 threshold, registration becomes mandatory. You must charge 5% TPS and 9.975% TVQ on all applicable sales to clients located in Quebec.

Incorporation vs. Sole Proprietorship

When starting out, almost all freelancers operate as Sole Proprietors (Travailleur autonome). It's simple: your business income is your personal income, taxed at your marginal personal tax rate. In Quebec, combined federal and provincial marginal tax rates can quickly reach 35% to 40% for middle-income earners.

As your revenue grows—typically when you are earning significantly more than you need for personal living expenses (often cited around the $90,000 - $100,000+ mark)—you might consider Incorporation. An incorporated business in Quebec benefits from a much lower corporate tax rate. This allows you to leave money inside the corporation to grow, paying personal tax only on the salary or dividends you withdraw. However, incorporation comes with higher accounting and legal fees, so it is not recommended for beginners.
