Coworking Spaces: How to Choose

Your ultimate guide to day passes, monthly memberships, essential amenities, and finding the perfect workspace anywhere in the world.

Passes vs. Memberships

Understanding the different access tiers is the first step to choosing the right coworking space. Here’s a breakdown of standard options:

Ideal for transients, slow travelers who mostly work from home, or those testing out a new space.

  • Pros: No commitment, maximum flexibility.
  • Cons: Often expensive per day. You don't get priority access to meeting rooms.

You pay for monthly access but do not have an assigned seat. You sit wherever is available.

  • Pros: Affordable monthly rate, great for networking as you move around.
  • Cons: Can be noisy; you might not get a good desk during peak hours; no place to leave your monitors/gear.

You have your own assigned desk in a shared area. It is yours 24/7.

  • Pros: You can leave monitors, keyboards, and personal items; guaranteed workspace; often includes locked storage.
  • Cons: More expensive than hot desking; you are still in a shared (potentially noisy) environment.

A fully enclosed, lockable office within the coworking building.

  • Pros: Ultimate privacy; great for teams; secure; quiet for endless calls.
  • Cons: Highly expensive; less chance for spontaneous networking.

Key Amenities Checklist

Not all coworking spaces are created equal. Use this checklist when touring or evaluating a space.

⚡

Pro Tip: Always run a speed test on your phone or laptop while touring a space. Do not rely solely on the "high-speed internet" claim on their website.

The Essentials (Non-Negotiable)

  • Rock-Solid Wi-Fi: Look for spaces with dedicated fiber lines and redundant backup connections. Ping should be low.
  • Ergonomic Seating: A basic wooden chair won't cut it for 8 hours. Look for adjustable, breathable office chairs.
  • Call Booths: If you take meetings, ensure there are soundproofed booths (and check if they require annoying booking systems or are walk-in).
  • Power Access: Abundant, easily accessible outlets at every single desk.

The "Nice-to-Haves"

  • 24/7 Access: Crucial if you work across different time zones. Check if this is standard or costs extra.
  • Free Coffee & Tea: Quality matters. A good espresso machine saves you $5/day.
  • Community Manager: A dedicated person who organizes events and introduces members.
  • Lockers: Helpful if you want to leave your gym bag or laptop overnight (often included with dedicated desks, but useful for hot deskers).
  • Monitors for Rent: Some spaces rent external monitors by the day/week.

The Rise of Global Coworking Memberships

For the truly nomadic professional, committing to a single location for a month isn't always practical. This has given rise to global roaming passes and aggregator networks. Services like WeWork All Access, Priority Pass for workspaces, and local regional networks allow you to pay a flat monthly fee and drop into hundreds of locations worldwide. When evaluating these, ensure their footprint aligns with your actual travel itinerary, as networks tend to be clustered heavily in major Western capitals and sparse in developing nomad hubs.

Why Ergonomics Matter for Remote Work

When selecting a coworking space, aesthetics often overshadow practical health considerations. However, poor ergonomics can lead to chronic back pain, carpal tunnel, and reduced productivity. A quality coworking space invests in proper lumbar support chairs, desks at the correct height, and sometimes even standing desks. If a space only offers rigid wooden cafe chairs or beanbags, it is designed for socializing or short-term drop-ins, not for serious, long-term deep work.

Corporate Sponsorships for Coworking

If you are a remote employee rather than a freelancer, check with your HR department about coworking stipends. Many forward-thinking remote companies now offer a monthly allowance (often ranging from $100 to $500) specifically earmarked for home office improvements or coworking space memberships. Providing your employer with data on how a dedicated workspace improves your uptime (thanks to backup internet) and mental health can often justify the approval of these expenses.
