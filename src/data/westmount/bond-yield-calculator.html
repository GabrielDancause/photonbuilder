Methodology

This bond yield calculator determines essential fixed income metrics using standard financial formulas:

Yield to Maturity (YTM)

YTM is the internal rate of return (IRR) of a bond if held to maturity, assuming all coupon payments are reinvested at the same rate. It is found by solving the following equation for y (the discount rate per period) using the Newton-Raphson numerical method:

Price = Σ [ Coupon / (1 + y)^t ] + [ Face Value / (1 + y)^n ]

Where t is the period, n is the total number of periods, and y is the periodic yield. The annualized YTM is calculated by multiplying the periodic yield by the payment frequency.

Current Yield

Current Yield = Annual Coupon Payment / Current Market Price.

Macaulay Duration

Macaulay duration is the weighted average time until all the bond's cash flows are received. The weights are the present value of each cash flow divided by the bond's total price. It is expressed in years.

Modified Duration & Convexity

Modified Duration = Macaulay Duration / (1 + Periodic YTM). It measures the approximate percentage change in a bond's price for a 100 basis point (1%) change in yield.

Convexity is a second-order derivative measure that accounts for the curvature of the price-yield relationship. It helps predict price changes more accurately than duration alone for larger shifts in interest rates.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do you calculate yield to maturity on a bond?
Yield to maturity (YTM) is calculated by solving the bond pricing equation for the discount rate that equates the present value of all future cash flows (coupons and principal) to the current market price of the bond. This is typically done using numerical methods like the Newton-Raphson or bisection method.
What is the difference between current yield and yield to maturity?
Current yield is simply the annual coupon payment divided by the current bond price, ignoring capital gains or losses at maturity. Yield to maturity (YTM) accounts for the total return, including interest payments and the difference between the current price and the face value received at maturity.
How does bond price affect yield?
Bond prices and yields have an inverse relationship. When a bond's price falls below its face value (discount), its yield increases. When the price rises above its face value (premium), its yield decreases.
What is modified duration in bond investing?
Modified duration measures a bond's price sensitivity to changes in interest rates. It represents the approximate percentage change in a bond's price for a 1% change in yield. A higher duration means the bond's price is more volatile when interest rates move.
Why is bond convexity important?
Convexity measures the curvature in the relationship between bond prices and yields, capturing how duration changes as yields change. It provides a more accurate estimate of price changes for larger shifts in interest rates, showing that bond prices rise more when yields fall than they drop when yields rise by the same amount.
