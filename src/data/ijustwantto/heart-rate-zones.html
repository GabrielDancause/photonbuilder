Understanding the Karvonen Method

The Karvonen formula is a mathematical equation that helps determine your target heart rate (HR) training zone. Unlike simple percentage-based calculations that only use your maximum heart rate, the Karvonen method incorporates your resting heart rate. This creates a more personalized and accurate reflection of your current fitness level.

By using your Heart Rate Reserve (HRR) — the difference between your maximum and resting heart rate — the formula calculates specific training zones tailored exactly to your cardiovascular capacity.

The 5 Heart Rate Training Zones

Training in different heart rate zones triggers different physiological adaptations in your body. Knowing which zone you are in helps you train more effectively for your specific goals.

  • Zone 1 (50-60%): Warm Up / Recovery. Used for warming up, cooling down, and active recovery. Training in this zone promotes blood flow and aids in recovery without stressing the body.
  • Zone 2 (60-70%): Fat Burn / Base Endurance. The foundation of endurance training. Your body relies primarily on fat for fuel. Long, steady sessions here build your aerobic base and improve capillary density.
  • Zone 3 (70-80%): Aerobic / Cardio. Improves cardiovascular efficiency and aerobic capacity. Your body begins to use more carbohydrates for energy. This zone is excellent for improving general fitness and stamina.
  • Zone 4 (80-90%): Anaerobic / Threshold. Training at or slightly below your lactate threshold. Workouts in this zone increase your body's ability to clear lactic acid, improving your speed and high-intensity endurance.
  • Zone 5 (90-100%): VO2 Max. Maximum effort training. Sustainable only for short bursts, this zone increases your fast-twitch muscle fiber recruitment and absolute maximum performance.

How to Find Your Resting and Max Heart Rate

For the most accurate results, you need to know your true resting and maximum heart rates.

Resting Heart Rate: Measure this first thing in the morning before getting out of bed. Take your pulse for 60 seconds. Do this for a few consecutive days and take the average.

Maximum Heart Rate: The standard formula (220 - your age) provides a rough estimate, but actual max HR can vary significantly due to genetics. For a more accurate number, consider a field test (like running a 5K at maximum effort and recording the highest HR) or a clinical stress test if cleared by your doctor.
